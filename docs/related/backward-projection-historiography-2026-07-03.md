# The "Century of Untested Assumption" in Linear A: An Evidence Report on the Prior Art and Historiography of the Backward Projection of Sound Values

## TL;DR
- The claim is **accurate with one important caveat**: for essentially a century the field has read Linear A with Linear B values as a working assumption defended philologically, but it has been quantitatively/empirically tested **only once** before — David Packard's 1974 "fictitious-decipherment" control study — and never with a pre-registered, held-out, multiplicity-corrected design. The defensible sentence is "argued philologically for a century but statistically tested only once (Packard 1974), and never with modern held-out/pre-registered methods."
- The single biggest risk to the researcher's framing is **overstating "never tested."** Packard 1974 is genuine prior quantitative art (his Ventris-value transfer beat fabricated decipherments by "just over 2:1," and ~3:1 context-adjusted, per Pope & Raison 1978). Referees will insist Packard be named as the partial precedent; the novelty lies in *design rigor* (pre-registration, held-out validation, multiplicity correction, external timestamping, leave-one-toponym-out), not in "being the first to test."
- The researcher's finding that the non-circular signal reduces essentially to place-name identifications is **strongly consonant with, but more quantitatively explicit than, the existing literature**: Steele & Meissner (2017), Salgarella, and others treat shared toponyms (pa-i-to = Phaistos, se-to-i-ja, su-ki-ri-ta = Sybrita) plus Cypriot-syllabary stability ("at least 10 or 11 signs") as the load-bearing independent evidence, but no one has previously *quantified* that essentially all non-shape signal collapses onto the toponym anchors.

## Key Findings

1. **The convention has a datable origin and a datable formalization.** Arthur Evans, in *Scripta Minoa* I (1909, p. 38), already observed that his Linear Class A and Class B "contain a large proportion of common elements" while noting Linear B added new signs. The value-transfer became operational only after the Ventris–Chadwick decipherment of Linear B (1953 *JHS* article; 1956 *Documents*). Ventris & Chadwick themselves flagged the risk, cautioning in *Documents* that "a wholesale reshuffling process" could have intervened between the two scripts. The transliteration convention was standardized editorially by Godart & Olivier's GORILA (1976–1985), which in volume 5 unified the numbering into the "AB" system (AB 01–AB 131) — a numbering that itself *presupposes* sign identity between A and B.

2. **The assumption was tested quantitatively exactly once before, by Packard (1974).** He built nine fictitious decipherments (randomly reassigning Linear B values among frequency-matched Linear A signs) plus a tenth using genuine Ventris values. Per van Soesbergen's standard account (*The Decipherment of Minoan Linear A*, Vol. II), the genuine transfer's "ratio of so-called confirmatory alternations is just over 2:1 in favour of decipherment by the Ventris's values over the average result of the fictitious decipherments," rising to "just over 3:1" context-adjusted (Pope & Raison 1978) and "5:1 for matches from the Knossos region." No subsequent study has performed a pre-registered, held-out, or permutation/cross-validated test of Linear A value recoverability.

3. **The assumption has been explicitly named a "problem" and defended, but rarely dissented from.** Steele & Meissner's 2017 chapter is titled — exact citation — "From Linear B to Linear A: The problem of the backward projection of sound values" (in P.M. Steele [ed.], *Understanding Relations Between Scripts: The Aegean Writing Systems*, Oxford/Philadelphia: Oxbow, 2017, pp. 93–110). It defends the projection using (a) Cypriot-syllabary stability ("at least 10 or 11 signs, and perhaps more depending on how far you are prepared to accept palaeographical" comparisons), (b) shared place names, (c) internal frequency/distribution, and (d) acrophony. Duhoux (1989) had earlier cautioned about the inference. No mainstream scholar argues the whole transfer is invalid; the debate is over *legitimacy and degree*.

4. **Reference works present the values as conventional/provisional, not established.** Palaima & Sikkenga (1999) repeatedly call Linear B values "provisional," "unknown," or "conventionally transcribed," and state flatly that "since Linear A has not been deciphered, we cannot identify its essential core." Salgarella's public formulation is that scholars "can 'read' the inscriptions" by applying homomorphic Linear B values "but we still cannot understand them," and that doing so "is not unproblematic."

5. **A clean historical parallel exists where shape identity did NOT preserve value: the Carian alphabet.** Most Carian letters resemble Greek letters but have sound values generally unrelated to the Greek ones; the decipherment (Ray 1981; Adiego & Schürr 1990s; confirmed by the 1996 Kaunos bilingual) required abandoning the assumption that Greek-looking letters had Greek values. This is the strongest cautionary analogue in the adjacent literature.

## Details

### (i) Chronological history of the convention

- **1909 — Evans, *Scripta Minoa* I, p. 38.** Evans classified the Cretan linear scripts into Class A and Class B and observed that they "contain a large proportion of common elements," while recognizing Linear B contained new signs (i.e., was not simply an evolved Linear A). This establishes the *shape-identity* observation a century before the researcher's test, but predates any sound values (Linear B was undeciphered).
- **1953 — Ventris & Chadwick, "Evidence for Greek Dialect in the Mycenaean Archives," *JHS* 73, 84–103.** The decipherment of Linear B supplied, for the first time, sound values that could in principle be projected backward. Ventris & Chadwick, *Documents in Mycenaean Greek* (1956; 2nd ed. 1973), p. 39, cautioned that "a wholesale reshuffling process" could have intervened — i.e., they explicitly noted the projection was not safe.
- **1953–1973 — Emmett Bennett and the Wingspread Convention.** Bennett's conventions (and his *A Minoan Linear B Index*, 1953) governed Linear B sign numbering; the Wingspread Convention explicitly concerned Linear B only. Bennett's own caution on the transfer is preserved in Ventris & Chadwick, *Documents* (2nd ed. 1973, p. 37): "where the same sign is used in both Linear A and B there is no guarantee that the same value is assigned to it." Earlier Linear A numbering systems (Stefani, Pugliese Carratelli, Brice, Raison, Pope) were separate.
- **1967–1974 — Packard.** Harvard PhD (1967), articles, and the monograph *Minoan Linear A* (Berkeley–Los Angeles–London, 1974). The first — and until recently only — quantitative test of whether graphic identity reflects phonic identity (see section iii).
- **1976–1985 — GORILA (Godart & Olivier, *Recueil des inscriptions en linéaire A*, 5 vols.).** The standard corpus. Its volume 5 (index/tables) abandoned the older Linear A numbering and adopted a unified "AB" numbering (AB 01–AB 131) for signs judged homographic across A and B — explicitly cross-referencing to Bennett's Linear B system. GORILA is a corpus edition; the *reading* convention (assigning phonetic values) was popularized especially by John Younger's online "Linear A Texts in Phonetic Transcription" (2000–), which is based on GORILA and improved with his own readings.
- **2017 — Steele & Meissner** name it "the backward projection of sound values" and treat it as a *problem* requiring defense.

### (ii) Typology of scholarly positions

- **Assumed (working convention):** GORILA editors (Godart & Olivier) via the AB numbering; John Younger's transliterated corpus (2000–, based on GORILA); the SigLA database (Salgarella & Castellano), which plans transliterations "using (approximate) phonetic values (based on comparison with Linear B)."
- **Defended explicitly:** Steele & Meissner (2017, *Understanding Relations Between Scripts*, pp. 93–110); their companion paper (2017, "Linear A and Linear B: Structural and contextual concerns," *Aegean Scripts*) states the analysis "has as its basis an assumption that such a view … must be correct: specifically that in the creation of Linear B the basic sign values of Linear A were retained with little modification." Earlier persuasive arguers they cite: Hooker 1975; Olivier 1975; Pope & Raison 1978; Godart 1984; Duhoux 1989.
- **Caveated:** Ventris & Chadwick (1956, p. 39, "wholesale reshuffling"); Bennett (in *Documents* 2nd ed. 1973, p. 37, "no guarantee"); Palaima & Sikkenga (1999, "provisional," "conventionally transcribed," "we cannot identify its essential core"); Salgarella (2020; 2025 Cambridge Element *Writing in Bronze Age Crete: 'Minoan' Linear A*), who calls the practice "not unproblematic" and stresses "read but not understand"; Duhoux (1989, p. 72, suggesting the statistics might even point toward *more* vowel phonemes in Minoan).
- **Tested (quantitatively):** Packard (1974) — the sole prior instance.
- **Dissented / resisted:** No mainstream scholar rejects the transfer wholesale. Fringe "decipherers" (Revesz's West-Ugric; van Soesbergen's Hurrian; various others) reject or rebuild the value grid, but the mainstream regards these as unaccepted. The scholarly "dissent" is confined to specific signs and to the o-/e-vowel series (whether homomorphic signs were value-reassigned).

### (iii) Prior quantitative/statistical tests — verdict on novelty

- **Packard 1974 is the closest and essentially the only prior art.** As described by van Soesbergen (quoting the standard account): "The question as to whether graphic identity of Linear A and B signs does or does not reflect phonic identity was tested with the help of computer methods by D.W. Packard… He constructed nine fictitious decipherments redistributing the Linear B phonetic values among the Linear A signs… In a tenth 'decipherment' he used the Ventris's values… the ratio of so-called confirmatory alternations is just over 2:1 in favour of decipherment by the Ventris values over the average result of the fictitious decipherments." Pope & Raison (1978, *Études minoennes* I, 24–25) re-analyzed and got ~3:1 with context adjustment (and 5:1 for the Knossos-region matches).
- **Everything since re-analyzes Packard or studies frequencies for other ends.** Duhoux (1989, p. 72) reinterprets Packard's statistics. Davis (2014) computed vowel frequencies (a 39.3%, i 25.7%, u 18.1%, e 14%, o 2.9%) but to argue about Minoan phonology, not to validate the transfer. Facchetti (1999, *Kadmos* 38) did statistical/morphematic work but not a transfer test.
- **Computational decipherment papers did NOT test the A-value transfer.** Luo, Cao & Barzilay (2019) used Linear B→Greek (a solved case) and Ugaritic, not Linear A value recovery. The Corazza/Ferrara/Tamburini/Valério group's Sign2Vec (PLOS ONE 2022) addressed Cypro-Minoan sign *classification*, not Linear A value transfer; Corazza et al. (2021) assigned Linear A *fraction* values, not syllabic values. Revesz's papers *assume/rebuild* a grid to argue for Uralic/West-Ugric affinities rather than validating the standard values.
- **Verdict:** The claim "never independently, empirically, or statistically tested until very recently" is **an overclaim as literally stated** because Packard (1974) was exactly such a test in kind. It becomes **defensible and strong** when narrowed to: never tested with a *pre-registered, held-out, multiplicity-corrected, externally timestamped* design, and never with a *leave-one-toponym-out* robustness analysis. Those design features are genuinely absent from the entire prior literature, including Packard.

### (iv) The place-name anchor evidence base

- The load-bearing toponym equations are pa-i-to = Phaistos, se-to-i-ja (unattested later, location debated — Siteia/Petras or eastern Crete), su-ki-ri-ta = Sybrita, plus personal names da-i-pi-ta, pa-ra-ne, and morphological pairs (Linear A qa-qa-ru ~ Linear B qa-qa-ro; A di-de-ru ~ B di-de-ro). Younger's toponym class also lists ku-do-ni = Kydonia, di-ka-te = Dikte, i-da = Ida, tu-ri-so = Tylissos, wi-na-du = Linear B (w)inato.
- Steele & Meissner (2017; companion 2017) treat these as key independent corroboration: "the consistency in the use of *to* in these place names strongly suggests… closely matching values." They pair them with Cypriot-syllabary stability ("at least 10 or 11 signs": a, i, da/ta, na, pa, po, ro/lo, sa, se, ti, to).
- Multiple sources converge on the point that place names/personal names shared across A and B "have been taken as evidence for shared sound values," and that this is effectively the substitute for the missing bilingual (there is no Rosetta Stone for Minoan). This is exactly the pressure point the researcher's Phase-2 anchor expansion targets.
- Gareth Owens's toponym-heavy readings are regarded as outside the mainstream; his work is often cited in popular media but treated with caution by specialists.

### (v) Counter-evidence and disputed cases

- **Carian** is the canonical parallel where homomorphy did not preserve value (see Key Findings 5).
- **The o-vowel series** is the internal locus of doubt: seven Linear B o-signs (do, jo, mo, no, qo, so, wo) have no attested Linear A predecessor, and the reassignment hypothesis (Palaima & Sikkenga) holds that some homomorphic signs may have had different values in A. Steele & Meissner argue against wholesale reassignment.
- **Coverage numbers vary by authority.** Per Steele & Meissner ("Linear A and Linear B: Structural and contextual concerns," 2017), Ventris & Chadwick's *Documents* table "identify 53 out of 89 signs as shared between Linear A and Linear B, a 60% identity. By 2005 … this figure had risen to 64 out of 89, giving a figure of 72%." The *securely valued* subset is much smaller — on the order of the ~10–11 Cypriot-confirmed signs plus the toponym-confirmed signs. Popular summaries claim "about 90% can be read," but that conflates "assignable a conventional value" with "securely valued." Younger grades values by confidence in his corpus.

### (vi) 2020–2026 publications bearing directly on this

- Salgarella, *Aegean Linear Script(s)* (CUP, 2020) and *Writing in Bronze Age Crete: 'Minoan' Linear A* (Cambridge Elements, 2025); Salgarella (2019) "Drawing lines," *Kadmos* 58.
- Steele, *Exploring Writing Systems and Practices in the Bronze Age Aegean* (Oxbow, 2023/2024), ch. 1 "How do we know that Linear B adopted Linear A syllabographic sign values with little change?"
- del Freo & Perna (eds.), *Manuale di epigrafia micenea* (2016; 2019) — ch. 5 on Linear A by M. Perna.
- Corazza, Tamburini, Valério & Ferrara, PLOS ONE (2022) — Cypro-Minoan classification (not A-value transfer). Nepal & Perono Cacciafoco (2024) and the "systematic review" in *Computational Linguistics* 50(2) (2024) survey computational approaches but confirm no held-out transfer validation.
- **No prior work matching the researcher's Phase-1 design was found.** The nearest is Packard (1974) in *kind*, and Steele & Meissner (2017) in *philological synthesis*.

## Recommendations

1. **Reframe the headline claim precisely.** Do not write "never tested." Write: *"For roughly a century the field has read Linear A with Linear B values as a working assumption, argued philologically (Hooker 1975; Pope & Raison 1978; Steele & Meissner 2017) and tested quantitatively only once, by Packard (1974), whose fictitious-decipherment controls showed the genuine transfer beat fabricated ones by just over 2:1 (~3:1 context-adjusted). No prior study has evaluated the recoverability of Linear A values with a pre-registered, held-out, multiplicity-corrected design."* This is bulletproof.
2. **Cite Packard prominently and generously as the intellectual ancestor,** then differentiate on method. Your genuine novelties: pre-registration, held-out validation, multiplicity correction, external timestamping, and leave-one-toponym-out robustness. Frame your contribution as the first *modern-statistical-standard* test, not the first test.
3. **Anchor the "problem" framing on Steele & Meissner (2017)** — they supply the field's own term ("backward projection") and its own admission that this is an assumption. Quote their companion paper's line that the analysis rests on "an assumption that such a view … must be correct." Reinforce with Bennett's "no guarantee" caution (Ventris & Chadwick 1973, p. 37).
4. **Make the place-name collapse your central positive result** and connect it explicitly to the existing consensus (toponyms + Cypriot stability as the independent evidence). Your quantification — that the non-circular signal reduces essentially to the toponym anchors — is the genuinely new empirical claim; state it as *strengthening and formalizing* what Steele & Meissner and Salgarella already suspected.
5. **Use Carian as your typological counterexample** to justify why the test is necessary (homomorphy ≠ homophony is empirically attested, not hypothetical).
6. **Thresholds that would change the framing:** If a referee produces any pre-2025 held-out or permutation test of Linear A values, downgrade "first modern test" to "independent replication." If the Salgarella 2025 Element or del Freo & Perna already quantifies the toponym dependence, cite it and reframe your result as confirmation rather than discovery.

## Caveats
- **Packard page ranges differ across sources** (pp. 112–114 vs 113–115); verify against the 1974 monograph directly before citing an exact page.
- **Evans's exact wording on *Scripta Minoa* I p. 38** ("large proportion of common elements") is verified via Palaima & Sikkenga (1999, n. 4) but not independently against the 1909 original image; confirm on the Heidelberg digitization (digi.ub.uni-heidelberg.de/diglit/evans1909) before quoting Evans verbatim.
- **Palaima & Sikkenga (1999) do not use the phrase "backward projection"** and do not frame a reading-convention caveat; their provisionality language concerns Linear B's extra signs and Linear A's undeciphered status. Use them for "conventional/provisional status," not for the "problem" framing (which belongs to Steele & Meissner).
- **"90% readable" claims** come from popular/secondary sources and conflate "assignable a conventional value" with "securely valued"; do not cite them as scholarly coverage figures.
- **The negative finding on prior held-out tests is "absence of evidence"** from exhaustive search; a fully defensible novelty claim should also check the bibliographies of Duhoux (1989) and Salgarella (2020) directly.
- Some numeric details (Duhoux's reported "3-sign ~81% / 4-sign ~99% sequence identity" statistics) could **not be verified** against a primary source and should be checked in Duhoux (1989) directly before use.
