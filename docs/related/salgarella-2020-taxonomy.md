# Salgarella 2020 — the A–B sign-relationship taxonomy

**Source:** Ester Salgarella, *Aegean Linear Script(s): Rethinking the Relationship between
Linear A and Linear B*, Cambridge University Press 2020, DOI 10.1017/9781108783477.
Local copy: `corpus/bronze/salgarella_2020/` (per-chapter PDFs with text layers).
All page numbers below are **book pages**. Machine-readable companion:
`experiments/crossscript_gate/salgarella_2020_grades.json`.

**Where the scheme lives.** Salgarella's sign-relationship taxonomy is split across two
places, plus an index that fixes her AB-number↔value correspondences:

1. **Ch. 1.4 "Linear A and Linear B: a First Assessment" (pp. 32–41)** — the sign-level
   grading. Tables 2–4 (pp. 35–37: *LB core syllabary*, *LB additional syllabary*, *LB
   undeciphered syllabograms*) carry a two-colour shading that is her per-sign
   homomorphy/homophony grade (key printed on p. 34).
2. **Ch. 4.1 "Structural Model" (pp. 283–356)** — the system-level taxonomy: the AB
   'core' vs. subsets model (§4.1.2, pp. 284–7, with Table 24 p. 286), the AB core's
   systematisation (§4.1.3, pp. 288–90), the 'B'-subset with its innovation categories
   (§4.1.4.1, pp. 291–300) and the 'A'-subsets (§4.1.4.2, pp. 300–55).
3. **Index of Signs Cited (pp. 412–16)** — tripartition *"Signs shared between Linear A
   and B"* (p. 412) / *"Linear A-only signs"* (p. 413) / *"Linear B-only signs"* (p. 415),
   and the only place she systematically prints AB-number/value pairs (e.g. "AB 01/da",
   "AB 76/ra2"; undeciphered signs printed with no value, e.g. "AB *56", "AB *79").

Ch. 2's classifications (GORILA formal classification §2.1.2.1 pp. 49–50; Schoep's
functional classification §2.1.2.2 pp. 50–1; her own alternative LA classification §2.2.1
pp. 52–4) are *LA-internal* taxonomies (simple vs. composite; phonetic /
semato-phonetic / sematographic / supra-structural functions, Figure 9 p. 52) — context
for, but not, the A–B correspondence scheme.

## (a) Classification scheme: labels and definitions

### Sign-level grading (Tables 2–4, pp. 35–37; key p. 34)

Her grading key, verbatim (p. 34):

> "The above results are summarised in Tables 2–4, with **LA-LB homomorphic
> syllabograms** highlighted in **light blue**, while **homomorphic and also likely
> homophone** syllabograms in **dark blue**."

So three effective grades for LB syllabograms vis-à-vis LA:

| grade | realisation | meaning |
|---|---|---|
| *homomorphic and also likely homophone* | dark-blue cell | shape shared with LA **and** LB value likely valid for LA |
| *homomorphic* | light-blue cell | shape shared with LA; homophony not individually supported |
| (unshaded) | no fill | no LA parallel — new LB introduction (cf. p. 38) |

The underlying principle, verbatim (p. 32): "sign homomorphy is likely to imply
homophony, at least in some instances and with an approximation." Her four supporting
arguments (pp. 32–3): "i) context of development of LB and its derivation from LA; ii)
LA-LB shared sign sequences; iii) retention of some LA logograms and use of the
acrophonic principle; iv) evidence for sign stability across the Aegean scripts."

Table 4 (p. 37), *"LB undeciphered syllabograms"*, lists 15 signs with no value: \*18,
\*19, \*22, \*34 (=\*35), \*47, \*49, \*56, \*63, \*64, \*65, \*79, \*82, \*83, \*86, \*89.

### System-level taxonomy (Structural Model, ch. 4.1)

Verbatim formulae (p. 284):

> "LB = AB (shared 'core') + B (subset: a language-specific component)"
> "LAs = A (shared 'core') + x/y/z… (site-specific sign subsets)"

Definitions, verbatim (p. 283): "The former are called **'AB' signs** since shared with LA
and, although in LB these signs were slightly modified, comparable variants are still
detectable. The latter [**B-only signs**] represent new introductions in LB and appear to
be language-bound (Greek only)."

Table 24 (p. 286, *LA and LB constitutive components*): core — "Distribution: Shared;
Constituents: Simple signs" (LAs: "AB signs + A signs"; LB: "AB signs"); subsets —
LAs "Distribution: Site-specific; Constituents: Simple + Composite signs but cross-site
configurations"; LB "'B'-subset = language-specific; Functional subdivision: Syllabic
subset (simple signs) – Logographic subset (simple and composite signs)".

B-subset innovation categories, verbatim (p. 291):

> "New signs introduced in the B-subset belong to the following categories:
> a) o-series: do, jo, mo, no, qo, so, wo
> b) e-series: pe
> c) complex/doublets: a2, ra3, ro2, dwe, dwo, two, pte
> d) undeciphered: \*19, \*63, \*64, \*83, \*89"

She further splits new-sign origins into "a source-driven development or an independent
creation" (p. 291), grading e.g. Melena's LA ancestors for do/jo as "more convincing"
and for no/so as "less convincing" (Figure 33, p. 292).

## (b) Headline counts, as printed

- **"High percentage of shared signs (72% sign identity)"** — p. 32 (feature (c) of her
  first assessment; her n. 90 credits Steele and Meissner 2017 for the figure). This is
  the count she carries; she prints no competing total of her own.
- **"some twenty signs are taken as new introductions in LB"** — p. 38.
- **"In the core syllabary seven o-series signs and pe do not seem to have parallel in the
  LA; in the additional syllabary this is the case for three out of the four additional
  a-vowel sounds (a2 /ha/, a3 /ai/, ra3 /rai/) and some labialised (dwe, dwo, twe, two)
  and yodised (ro2, pte?) signs."** — p. 38.
- **Cypriot-stable signs** (shape + same/closely related value across LB and the Cypriot
  Syllabary): "a, i, da (corresponding to ta in the Cypriot Syllabary), na, pa, po, ro
  (corresponding to lo in the Cypriot Syllabary, but the sign represents both ro/lo in
  LB), sa, se, ti, to." — p. 34 (11 signs; her n. 97 → Steele and Meissner 2017, table 6.2).
- **GORILA numbering ranges** (p. 54–5): simple signs "AB 01–87, A 100–371,
  A 400VAS–418VAS"; composite signs "A 501–664 … unique to LA (with one
  exception, MA+RU, retained in LB)".
- **Derived, not printed** (tallied by us from the cell fills of her tables — Salgarella
  prints no numeric totals for the shading): Table 2 (p. 35): **25 dark-blue**
  (homomorphic + likely homophone), **27 light-blue** (homomorphic), **8 unshaded**
  (do, jo, mo, no, pe, qo, so, wo). Table 3 (p. 36): 7 light-blue (a3, au, nwa, pu2, ra2,
  ta2?, twe?), 7 unshaded (a2, dwe, dwo, pte?, ra3, ro2, two). Table 4 (p. 37): 15
  undeciphered. Extraction method: PDF fill colours (dark = CMYK 0.495/0.287/0/0,
  light = CMYK 0.172/0.084/0.003/0), cross-checked against the page images.

These supersede the older 64-of-89/72% framings in repo docs **only in attribution**:
Salgarella 2020 prints "72% sign identity" (p. 32) and attributes it to Steele & Meissner
2017; she prints no "64 of 89" figure anywhere we searched (ch. 1.4, ch. 4.1, Index).

## (c) Her position on value transfer / backward projection

Verbatim quotes:

1. p. 32: "sign homomorphy is likely to imply homophony, at least in some instances and
   with an approximation. A number of arguments have been put forward in support of the
   validity of this equivalence, which, however, have to be used with due caution."
2. p. 34: "as clearly pointed out by Steele and Meissner, in doing so we try to explain an
   earlier situation (i.e. LA phonology) with a later one (i.e. LB phonology), which is
   not a methodologically straightforward procedure. Nevertheless, the overall weight of
   the arguments provided would favour the reliability of applying this method, thus
   allowing to read LA with at least approximate phonetic values."
3. p. 374: "i) phonetic continuity, in view of the legitimacy of the backward projection
   of LB sound values;" — with the caveat in n. 2 (p. 374): "Although most scholars see
   this as the most plausible scenario (esp. Steele and Meissner 2017), we cannot be
   entirely sure this was always the case."

**Classification (assumed / defended / caveated / tested): defended-with-caveat.** She
argues the transfer positively from four explicit lines of evidence (pp. 32–3) and calls
the backward projection legitimate (p. 374), while flagging the direction-of-inference
problem (p. 34) and residual uncertainty (p. 374 n. 2). She does not *test* the values
against held-out data; the approximation ("at least approximate phonetic values", p. 34)
is a standing qualifier.

## (d) Lexical identifications she endorses

All on p. 33 (ch. 1.4), presented as evidence for homophony, not as decipherments:

- **Toponyms:** "pa-i-to 'Phaistos', se-to-i-ja, su-ki-ri-ta" — "LA and LB share a number
  of sign sequences, often interpretable as toponyms or anthroponyms".
- **Anthroponyms:** "da-i-pi-ta, pa-ra-ne"; and morphologically adapted pairs "LA
  anthroponyms qa-qa-ru and di-de-ru turned into LB qa-qa-ro and di-de-ro". Her
  endorsement strength: "These name equations are a strong piece of evidence providing
  reasonably secure readings of the homomorphic signs contained in the sequences."
  (n. 93 defers the complete list to Steele and Meissner 2017, table 6.3.)
- **MA+RU 'wool':** "a monogram made up with the syllabograms MA and RU and likely
  to represent the Minoan word for wool (maru/malu), borrowed into Greek as μαλλός
  'wool/fleece'" (p. 33; also pp. 53, 55: "with one exception, MA+RU, retained in LB").
- **NI 'fig' (acrophonic):** "the first syllable of the supposedly Minoan word for 'fig'
  nikyleon … therefore read as 'ni' by the acrophonic principle" (p. 33).

No other LA lexical readings are endorsed; her project is script-structural, and she
explicitly avoids language identification claims.
